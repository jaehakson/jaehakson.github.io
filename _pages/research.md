A huge proportion of eukaryotic genomes consists of noncoding DNA. While once termed “junk DNA”, much of this sequence is now known to play critical roles in gene regulation, genome structure, and disease. Despite growing evidence for crucial roles of non-coding sequences in genome functions, as the blueprint for development and life operation, the relationship between the noncoding DNA and genomes has not been completely illuminated: why do genomes carry harmful genetic entities, and are regulatory sequence changes driving expression differences responsible for genomic changes and species-specific phenotypes? 

## The host-transposons intragenomic conflicts

Transposable elements (TEs) are selfish genetic entities that move around and change their locations within the host genome. The TE mobilization often cause host genome instability via deletions, duplications, and rearrangements, and is responsible for a huge number of deleterious phenotypes in animals. Hosts have in turn evolved a variety of TE-silencing mechanisms to suppress TEs, including small RNA mediated silencing pathways such as piRNAs. Despite the host defense systems, TEs have maintained a protracted evolutionary presence within host genomes. During the co-evolution over deep time, some TEs have evolved counter-defense (anti-silencing) mechanisms to evade host suppression and promote their proliferation within host genomes. The TE-host intragenomic conflicts therefore form an evolutionary arms race with TEs evolving to escape host silencing and the host genome evolving to regulate TEs. My research focuses on co-evolution between TEs and their host silencing system to understand how the TE-host interactions influence phenotypic novelty and diseases via genome integrity and organization.

## Mechanisms that drive sex chromosome evolution and turnover

Sexual reproduction is ubiquitous and sexual developments are essential processes in eukaryotic organisms; however, mechanisms that determine sex are highly variable across taxa. Changes in sex determinations often drive sex chromosome transitions, resulting in the formation of new and young sex chromosomes. A huge diversity of sex chromosome systems exist in nature, indicating that sex chromosomes have evolved independently and rapidly many times. A canonical prediction of the sex chromosome evolution begins a pair of homologous autosomes acquiring a new sex determining locus, each of which becomes the new X and Y (or Z and W) chromosomes. Recent advances in genomic techniques enabled the identification of the new and young sex chromosomes in multiple taxa. How the sex chromosomes are differentiated at the very early stages from each other remains elusive. My research address this question by studying why sex chromosome turnover happens, how sequence and expression divergence between sex chromosomes occur, and what are the underlying patterns and processes for the sex chromosome evolution.
